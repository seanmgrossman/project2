Central Florida Birds

Anhinga
Anhinga
Anhinga anhinga

A bird you would normally see sunning near bodies of water, the Anhinga is known as the Water-Turkey for its swimming habits and broad tail, and also as the Snake-Bird for its habit of swimming with just its long head and neck sticking out of the water.

Black Vulture
Black Vulture
Coragyps atratus

Black Vultures are compact birds with broad wings, short tails, and powerful wingbeats. Normally seen next to a carcass, or flying up above looking for a new meal. They share food with relatives, feeding young for months after theyâ€™ve fledged.

Great Egret
Great Egret
Ardea alba

Found near water, though it sometimes looks in bushes for food. They normally hunt standing immobile or wading through wetlands to capture the prey with a deadly jab of their yellow bill.

Little Blue Heron
Little Blue Heron
Egretta caerulea

Little Blue Herons stalk shallow waters for small fish and amphibians, adopting a quiet, methodical approach.They nest in trees, usually among other nesting herons and wading birds. In those trees , they build stick nests in trees alongside other colonial waterbirds.

Mottled Duck
Mottled Duck
Anas fulvigula

The only duck adapted to breeding in southern marshes, the Mottled Duck is a dull relative of the Mallard. They are the native common duck found in Florida, unlike the invasive duck Muscovy Duck.

Northern Mockingbird
Northern Mockingbird
Mimus polyglottos

Northern Mockingbirds sing almost endlessly, even sometimes at night. They commonly mimic other birds, hence the name. They are the state bird of Florida. If birds intrude on their territories they will harass them till they leave.

Red-shouldered Hawk
Red-shouldered Hawk
Buteo lineatus

Red-shouldered Hawk is marked with barred reddish-peachy underbelly and a strongly banded tail. In flight, they search for food letting out loud calls that can be heard from the ground. These hawks hunt prey ranging from mice to frogs and snakes.

Sandhill Crane
Sandhill Crane
Ardea alba

Sandhill Cranes group together with others, filling the air with distinctive rolling cries that echo wherever they go. You typically see them paired together with at least one other crane. Mates display to each other with exuberant dances.

White Ibis
White Ibis
Eudocimus albus

Often found on grass and or near bodies of water, the White Ibis peaks down with a long beak for food. Primarily it eats a diet of large insects, and other small invertebrates. When they breed, they group up in large colonies with other birds.

Wood Stork
Wood Stork
Mycteria americana

The Wood Stork is seen near bodies of water when not flying. It iis the only stork that breeds in the United States. It breeds in late winter to be timed with the Florida dry season. It eats fish that become concentrated in shrinking pools due to these dry spells.
